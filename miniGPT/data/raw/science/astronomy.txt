Astronomy is the scientific study of objects and phenomena beyond Earth.

It studies planets, moons, stars, galaxies, nebulae, black holes, and the universe as a whole.

Astronomy uses observations, mathematics, physics, and technology to understand the cosmos.

The universe contains matter, energy, space, and time.

The observable universe is the portion of the universe whose light or other information can reach us.

Because light travels at a finite speed, observing distant objects means looking into the past.

The farther away an object is, the older the light we receive from it may be.

A light-year is a unit of distance.

It is the distance light travels in one year.

A parsec is another astronomical unit of distance.

Astronomical distances are often extremely large.

The Sun is the star at the center of our solar system.

The solar system contains the Sun and objects gravitationally bound to it.

The planets orbit the Sun.

The eight planets are Mercury, Venus, Earth, Mars, Jupiter, Saturn, Uranus, and Neptune.

The inner planets are generally rocky.

The outer planets are much larger and include gas giants and ice giants.

Mercury is the closest planet to the Sun.

Venus has a thick atmosphere and an extremely hot surface.

Earth has liquid surface water and supports known life.

Mars is a rocky planet with a thin atmosphere.

Jupiter is the largest planet in the solar system.

Saturn is known for its prominent ring system.

Uranus rotates with an extreme axial tilt.

Neptune is a distant ice giant with powerful winds.

A planet orbits a star and has sufficient mass for gravity to shape it approximately into a round form.

The exact scientific classification of a planet depends on the adopted definition.

Dwarf planets also orbit the Sun and are approximately round but do not meet all the criteria used for full planet classification.

Pluto is classified as a dwarf planet.

Asteroids are rocky or metallic objects that orbit the Sun.

Many asteroids are found in the asteroid belt between Mars and Jupiter.

Comets contain significant amounts of ice and dust.

When a comet approaches the Sun, heating can cause material to escape and form a coma and tail.

The tail of a comet generally points away from the Sun because of solar radiation and the solar wind.

Meteoroids are relatively small objects moving through space.

A meteor is the visible streak produced when a meteoroid enters an atmosphere and heats the surrounding gas.

A meteorite is a fragment that reaches the ground.

The Moon is Earth's natural satellite.

The Moon orbits Earth.

The Moon does not produce its own visible light.

It reflects sunlight.

The phases of the Moon result from the changing geometry of the Sun, Earth, and Moon.

A lunar eclipse occurs when Earth passes between the Sun and Moon in an appropriate alignment.

A solar eclipse occurs when the Moon passes between the Sun and Earth in an appropriate alignment.

The Moon's gravity contributes to Earth's ocean tides.

Tides are also influenced by the Sun and the geometry of the Earth-Moon system.

Gravity is a fundamental force that influences astronomical motion.

Objects with mass attract one another gravitationally.

The strength of gravitational attraction depends on mass and distance.

Orbital motion occurs when an object moves under the influence of gravity.

An orbit is a path determined by motion and gravitational forces.

An orbit does not require a planet to constantly use an engine to remain in space.

An orbiting object is continuously falling toward the central body while moving sideways.

The combination of motion and gravity produces the orbital path.

A satellite is an object that orbits another object.

Natural satellites include moons.

Artificial satellites are built and launched by humans.

Artificial satellites support communication, navigation, weather observation, science, and Earth monitoring.

A star is a massive object composed primarily of hot plasma.

Stars produce energy through nuclear processes.

The Sun is a typical star in some respects, although stars vary greatly in mass, temperature, age, and composition.

Stars form in large clouds of gas and dust.

These clouds are called molecular clouds or stellar nurseries.

Gravity can cause regions of a cloud to collapse.

As material collapses, the central region becomes denser and hotter.

A protostar forms before sustained hydrogen fusion begins.

When the core becomes sufficiently hot and dense, nuclear fusion can begin.

During the main sequence stage, many stars fuse hydrogen into helium in their cores.

The energy released by fusion helps support the star against gravitational collapse.

The balance between inward gravity and outward pressure is important in a star.

The mass of a star strongly influences its evolution.

Massive stars generally burn their fuel faster than less massive stars.

A star's color is related to its surface temperature.

Blue stars are generally hotter than red stars.

The brightness of a star can be described using different measurements.

Apparent brightness describes how bright a star appears from Earth.

Intrinsic luminosity describes the total energy emitted by a star per unit time.

A nearby dim star can appear brighter than a distant luminous star.

A stellar spectrum contains information about a star.

Spectral lines can reveal chemical composition.

Spectral features can also provide information about temperature and motion.

The Doppler effect changes the observed wavelength of waves when the source and observer move relative to each other.

Astronomers can use Doppler shifts to study the motion of stars and galaxies.

A star eventually changes when its nuclear fuel changes.

A low- or medium-mass star can expand into a red giant.

The outer layers may eventually be expelled.

The remaining core can become a white dwarf.

A white dwarf is a dense stellar remnant.

A massive star can undergo a supernova explosion.

The core may collapse during the final stages of a massive star's evolution.

A neutron star is an extremely dense stellar remnant.

Some neutron stars rotate rapidly and emit beams of radiation.

A pulsar is a rotating neutron star whose radiation can appear as regular pulses to an observer.

If a sufficiently massive stellar core collapses, it can form a black hole.

A black hole is a region of spacetime from which no information can escape once it passes the event horizon according to our current physical theories.

The event horizon is a boundary associated with a black hole.

The center of a black hole is described by general relativity as a region where the theory predicts extreme conditions.

Black holes can have different masses.

Stellar-mass black holes can form from the collapse of massive stars.

Supermassive black holes can exist at the centers of galaxies.

Matter falling toward a black hole can form an accretion disk.

Friction and other processes in the disk can produce intense radiation.

Black holes can influence surrounding stars and gas through gravity.

A galaxy is a large system of stars, gas, dust, dark matter, and other components bound together by gravity.

The Milky Way is the galaxy containing our solar system.

Galaxies can have different shapes.

Spiral galaxies have disk-like structures with spiral arms.

Elliptical galaxies have more rounded or elongated shapes.

Irregular galaxies do not fit common regular classifications.

The Milky Way is a large spiral galaxy.

The solar system lies in one of the Milky Way's spiral regions.

The center of the Milky Way contains a supermassive black hole.

Galaxies can interact gravitationally.

A galaxy collision can distort their structures.

The stars within colliding galaxies do not necessarily collide with one another because the distances between stars are extremely large.

Galaxy clusters are groups of galaxies bound together by gravity.

Superclusters are larger structures containing groups and clusters of galaxies.

The large-scale structure of the universe includes enormous networks of galaxies and matter.

Dark matter is a form of matter inferred from gravitational effects.

Dark matter does not appear to interact strongly with light in the way ordinary matter does.

Its exact nature remains an active area of scientific research.

Dark energy is a term associated with the observed accelerated expansion of the universe.

Its physical nature is not fully understood.

The universe is expanding.

Distant galaxies generally appear to be moving away from one another on large scales.

The expansion of space is different from ordinary motion through space.

The cosmic microwave background is radiation left from the early universe.

It provides important evidence about the history and structure of the universe.

The Big Bang model describes the early hot, dense state and subsequent expansion of the universe.

The Big Bang was not an explosion into preexisting ordinary space in the simple everyday sense.

Space itself has expanded over cosmic history according to modern cosmological models.

The universe has evolved over billions of years.

The early universe contained mostly simple elements.

Stars later produced heavier elements through nuclear processes.

Supernovae and other stellar events distributed many elements into space.

Planets and other objects formed from material enriched by previous generations of stars.

Many elements found on Earth were produced in ancient stars or extreme cosmic events.

Exoplanets are planets orbiting stars outside the solar system.

Thousands of exoplanets have been discovered using different methods.

The transit method detects small changes in a star's brightness when a planet passes in front of it.

The radial velocity method detects motion of a star caused by the gravitational influence of an orbiting planet.

Other methods can directly or indirectly detect planets.

Some exoplanets may have conditions that could potentially support life.

Habitability depends on many factors.

Liquid water may be important for life as we know it.

Atmosphere, temperature, chemistry, radiation, and geological activity can also influence habitability.

The habitable zone is a region around a star where conditions may allow liquid water on a planet's surface under suitable atmospheric conditions.

Being in a habitable zone does not guarantee that a planet supports life.

Astrobiology studies the origin, evolution, distribution, and potential existence of life in the universe.

Scientists search for evidence of life in places such as Mars and icy moons.

Some moons of the outer solar system may contain subsurface oceans.

Life beyond Earth has not been confirmed.

The search for extraterrestrial intelligence examines possible signs of technological civilizations.

Radio signals and other forms of evidence can be studied.

No confirmed evidence of extraterrestrial intelligent life has been established.

Telescopes collect electromagnetic radiation.

Optical telescopes observe visible light.

Radio telescopes observe radio waves.

Infrared telescopes detect infrared radiation.

Ultraviolet, X-ray, and gamma-ray observatories study high-energy or short-wavelength phenomena.

Atmospheric absorption affects which wavelengths can be observed from Earth's surface.

Space telescopes can observe wavelengths blocked or weakened by Earth's atmosphere.

Astronomers use multiple wavelengths to study the same object.

Different wavelengths reveal different physical processes.

Spectroscopy analyzes the distribution of light by wavelength.

Spectroscopy can reveal chemical composition, temperature, density, and motion.

Astronomical images can be processed to reveal faint structures.

The colors in scientific astronomical images may represent wavelengths that are not visible to human eyes.

Space exploration involves sending spacecraft beyond Earth.

A spacecraft can be robotic or crewed.

Robotic spacecraft have explored planets, moons, asteroids, comets, and the outer solar system.

Space probes can collect measurements and transmit data back to Earth.

A rocket provides the thrust needed to overcome gravity and achieve the required trajectory.

Orbital velocity depends on the gravitational environment and desired orbit.

A spacecraft can use gravity assists to change its trajectory and speed.

A gravity assist uses the motion and gravity of a planet or moon.

Spacecraft must manage energy, communication, navigation, and thermal conditions.

The vacuum of space creates unique engineering challenges.

Spacecraft may experience extreme temperatures.

Radiation can damage electronics and biological systems.

Long-duration human spaceflight presents additional challenges.

Astronauts experience reduced gravity in orbit.

Long periods in microgravity can affect the human body.

Life-support systems provide oxygen, remove carbon dioxide, regulate temperature, and manage water.

Space science helps improve understanding of Earth.

Earth observation satellites monitor weather, climate, oceans, vegetation, and natural disasters.

Astronomy also helps improve technologies used in communication, navigation, imaging, and scientific measurement.

The universe contains structures on scales ranging from subatomic particles to galaxies and cosmic networks.

Astronomy connects physics, chemistry, geology, biology, mathematics, and engineering.

The study of astronomy helps humans understand the origin and evolution of stars, planets, galaxies, and the universe itself.

Astronomy continues to develop as new observations, instruments, and theories expand our understanding of the cosmos.
