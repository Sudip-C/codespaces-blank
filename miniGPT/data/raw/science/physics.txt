Physics is the study of matter, energy, motion, forces, space, and time.

The physical world contains objects, particles, fields, and energy.

Physics attempts to describe how these things behave.

A physical quantity is something that can be measured.

Examples include length, time, mass, temperature, speed, and energy.

A measurement consists of a numerical value and a unit.

The International System of Units is commonly called the SI system.

The meter is the SI unit of length.

The kilogram is the SI unit of mass.

The second is the SI unit of time.

The ampere is the SI unit of electric current.

The kelvin is the SI unit of temperature.

A scalar quantity has magnitude but no direction.

Examples of scalar quantities include mass, temperature, time, and energy.

A vector quantity has both magnitude and direction.

Examples include displacement, velocity, acceleration, and force.

Distance is the total length of a path traveled by an object.

Displacement is the change in position from an initial location to a final location.

Speed describes how quickly distance changes with time.

Velocity describes how quickly displacement changes with time.

Acceleration describes how quickly velocity changes with time.

An object can have constant speed while changing direction.

In that situation, its velocity changes because velocity includes direction.

Motion describes how an object's position changes over time.

A reference frame provides a perspective from which motion is measured.

An object may be stationary relative to one reference frame and moving relative to another.

Newton's laws describe important relationships between force and motion.

Newton's first law states that an object remains at rest or continues moving with constant velocity unless acted upon by a net external force.

This property is called inertia.

Mass measures an object's resistance to changes in motion.

Newton's second law relates force, mass, and acceleration.

The net force on an object equals its mass multiplied by its acceleration.

A larger force can produce greater acceleration for the same mass.

A larger mass requires more force to produce the same acceleration.

Newton's third law states that forces occur in interacting pairs.

When one object exerts a force on another object, the second object exerts an equal and opposite force on the first.

The two forces act on different objects.

Force is a vector quantity.

The SI unit of force is the newton.

Gravity is an attractive interaction between masses.

Near Earth's surface, objects accelerate downward due to Earth's gravitational field.

The acceleration due to Earth's gravity is approximately 9.8 meters per second squared near the surface.

The exact value varies slightly with location.

Mass and weight are different quantities.

Mass measures the amount of matter or resistance to acceleration.

Weight is the gravitational force acting on an object.

An object's mass remains approximately the same when it moves to another planet.

Its weight changes because the gravitational field may be different.

Friction is a force that opposes relative motion between surfaces.

Static friction can prevent an object from beginning to move.

Kinetic friction acts when surfaces slide relative to each other.

Friction can produce heat.

Friction can be useful.

Walking, braking, and gripping objects depend on friction.

Air resistance is a type of drag force.

Drag opposes motion through a fluid.

The amount of drag depends on factors such as speed, shape, and the properties of the fluid.

A free-body diagram represents the forces acting on an object.

Free-body diagrams help analyze motion.

The net force is the vector sum of all forces acting on an object.

If the net force is zero, an object can remain at rest or move with constant velocity.

Work describes energy transferred by a force acting through a displacement.

When a force causes an object to move in the direction of the force, work is performed.

The SI unit of work is the joule.

Energy is the capacity to cause change or perform work.

Kinetic energy is the energy associated with motion.

An object's kinetic energy depends on its mass and speed.

Potential energy is stored energy associated with position or configuration.

Gravitational potential energy depends on position in a gravitational field.

Elastic potential energy can be stored in stretched or compressed objects.

The law of conservation of energy states that energy cannot be created or destroyed in an isolated system.

Energy can be transferred or transformed between forms.

Chemical energy can become thermal energy.

Electrical energy can become light energy.

Potential energy can become kinetic energy.

The total energy of an isolated system remains constant.

Power describes the rate at which work is performed or energy is transferred.

The SI unit of power is the watt.

A higher-power device transfers energy more quickly.

Momentum is related to an object's mass and velocity.

Momentum is a vector quantity.

In an isolated system, total momentum is conserved.

Collisions can transfer momentum between objects.

An elastic collision conserves kinetic energy as well as momentum in the idealized case.

An inelastic collision conserves momentum but not necessarily kinetic energy.

Circular motion involves movement along a curved path.

An object moving in a circle can have constant speed while continuously changing velocity.

The change in velocity requires acceleration.

Centripetal acceleration points toward the center of circular motion.

A force directed toward the center can provide the centripetal force.

Rotation describes motion around an axis.

Torque describes the rotational effect of a force.

A force applied farther from a pivot can produce greater torque.

Angular momentum is associated with rotational motion.

Angular momentum is conserved in an isolated system when no external torque acts.

Pressure is force distributed over an area.

The same force can produce different pressure depending on the area over which it acts.

Fluids include liquids and gases.

Fluid pressure can change with depth.

Buoyancy is an upward force exerted by a fluid on an immersed object.

An object can float when the upward buoyant force balances its weight.

Density is mass per unit volume.

An object with lower average density than a fluid may float in that fluid.

Temperature describes the thermal state of matter.

Heat is energy transferred because of a temperature difference.

Heat naturally transfers from warmer objects to cooler objects until thermal equilibrium is approached.

Conduction transfers thermal energy through direct interactions between particles.

Convection transfers thermal energy through the movement of fluids.

Radiation transfers energy through electromagnetic waves.

Thermal energy is related to the microscopic motion of particles.

The temperature of an object is related to the average kinetic energy of its particles.

Different materials respond differently to heating.

Thermal expansion occurs when many materials increase in size as their temperature rises.

Phase changes include melting, freezing, vaporization, condensation, sublimation, and deposition.

During a phase change, energy can be transferred without a corresponding change in temperature under certain conditions.

The laws of thermodynamics describe relationships between heat, work, energy, and entropy.

The first law of thermodynamics expresses conservation of energy.

The second law describes the direction of natural processes and the behavior of entropy.

Entropy is associated with the number of possible microscopic arrangements of a system.

Natural processes tend to increase total entropy in an isolated system.

Waves transfer energy through oscillations or disturbances.

A wave can travel through a medium or, in some cases, through empty space.

Mechanical waves require a medium.

Sound is a mechanical wave.

Electromagnetic waves can travel through a vacuum.

A wave has properties including wavelength, frequency, amplitude, and speed.

Wavelength is the distance between corresponding points on successive waves.

Frequency is the number of cycles per unit time.

Amplitude describes the size of the oscillation.

The speed of a wave depends on the type of wave and the properties of the medium.

Sound is produced by vibrations.

Sound travels through a medium as a mechanical disturbance.

Sound cannot travel through a perfect vacuum.

The frequency of a sound is related to its perceived pitch.

The amplitude of a sound wave is related to its intensity.

Light is electromagnetic radiation.

Visible light is one region of the electromagnetic spectrum.

Other regions include radio waves, microwaves, infrared radiation, ultraviolet radiation, X-rays, and gamma rays.

Electromagnetic waves differ in wavelength and frequency.

The speed of light in vacuum is approximately 300,000 kilometers per second.

Reflection occurs when a wave bounces from a surface.

Refraction occurs when a wave changes direction as it enters a different medium.

Refraction occurs because the wave speed changes between media.

Diffraction is the spreading of waves around obstacles or through openings.

Interference occurs when waves overlap.

Constructive interference can increase amplitude.

Destructive interference can reduce amplitude.

Electric charge is a fundamental property of matter.

There are positive and negative charges.

Like charges repel.

Opposite charges attract.

Electric current is the flow of electric charge.

Voltage represents electric potential difference.

Resistance opposes the flow of electric current.

Ohm's law relates voltage, current, and resistance for certain materials and conditions.

Electrical power describes the rate of electrical energy transfer.

A circuit provides a path through which current can flow.

A closed circuit allows current to flow.

An open circuit interrupts the path.

Conductors allow charge to move relatively easily.

Insulators strongly resist charge movement.

Semiconductors have electrical properties between those of conductors and insulators.

Magnetism is associated with moving electric charges and magnetic materials.

Electric currents can produce magnetic fields.

Changing magnetic fields can induce electric currents.

This relationship forms the basis of electromagnetic induction.

Generators convert mechanical energy into electrical energy.

Motors convert electrical energy into mechanical energy.

Electromagnetism describes the relationship between electricity and magnetism.

Atoms consist of a nucleus surrounded by electrons.

The nucleus contains protons and neutrons.

Protons have positive electric charge.

Electrons have negative electric charge.

Neutrons have no net electric charge.

The number of protons determines the atomic number of an element.

Atoms of the same element can have different numbers of neutrons.

These forms are called isotopes.

Chemical elements are organized in the periodic table.

Atoms can interact to form molecules and compounds.

The behavior of matter depends on interactions between atoms and molecules.

Quantum mechanics describes physical behavior at very small scales.

Particles such as electrons can exhibit both particle-like and wave-like behavior.

Quantum systems can have discrete energy levels.

The act of measurement can affect certain quantum systems.

Quantum mechanics differs from classical physics in important ways.

Relativity describes relationships between space, time, motion, and gravity.

Special relativity applies to objects moving at high speeds and systems without gravity being treated as spacetime curvature.

The speed of light in vacuum is the same for all inertial observers.

Time and space measurements can depend on the relative motion of observers.

Mass and energy are related.

General relativity describes gravity as a consequence of the geometry of spacetime.

Mass and energy can influence spacetime geometry.

Objects move through curved spacetime according to this geometry.

Classical physics provides excellent approximations for many everyday situations.

Quantum physics is required for many microscopic phenomena.

Relativity becomes important at very high speeds or in strong gravitational fields.

Physics uses mathematical models to describe natural phenomena.

A model is an approximation of reality.

A useful model captures important behavior while simplifying unnecessary details.

Experiments test predictions made by physical theories.

Measurements contain uncertainty.

Experimental results should be interpreted with consideration of measurement uncertainty.

A scientific theory is a well-supported explanation of observed phenomena.

A scientific law describes a consistent relationship observed in nature.

Scientific models can be refined when new evidence becomes available.

Physics is closely connected to chemistry, biology, astronomy, engineering, and many other fields.

Engineering applies physical principles to design systems and devices.

Astronomy applies physics to understand stars, planets, galaxies, and the universe.

The goal of physics is to develop quantitative descriptions of how nature behaves.

Physics combines observation, experimentation, mathematics, and theoretical reasoning.

Understanding physics provides a foundation for explaining motion, matter, energy, technology, and the behavior of the universe.
