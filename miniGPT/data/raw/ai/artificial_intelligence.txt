Artificial intelligence is a field of computer science concerned with building computational systems that can perform tasks that traditionally require human intelligence. These tasks may include understanding language, recognizing patterns, interpreting images, making predictions, planning actions, solving problems, and learning from experience. An artificial intelligence system is not necessarily designed to imitate every aspect of human thinking. Instead, an AI system is usually designed to perform a particular task or group of tasks using algorithms, data, and computational resources.

The central idea behind artificial intelligence is to create systems that can transform information into useful decisions or actions. A traditional computer program generally follows rules written explicitly by a programmer. An AI system may instead learn patterns from examples. For instance, a conventional program might contain a specific rule for identifying a cat in an image, while a machine learning system can examine many examples of cats and learn statistical patterns associated with them.

Artificial intelligence includes many different areas of research. Machine learning focuses on systems that learn from data. Deep learning uses neural networks with multiple computational layers. Natural language processing focuses on the interaction between computers and human language. Computer vision enables machines to process and interpret images and video. Robotics combines software intelligence with physical machines. Reinforcement learning studies how an agent can learn to make decisions by interacting with an environment.

The development of artificial intelligence has been influenced by mathematics, statistics, computer science, neuroscience, linguistics, philosophy, and engineering. Mathematics provides tools for representing information and optimizing models. Statistics provides methods for reasoning about uncertainty and discovering patterns in data. Computer science provides algorithms and computational systems. Neuroscience has inspired some computational models, although artificial neural networks are highly simplified mathematical systems rather than direct copies of biological brains.

The history of artificial intelligence includes several major periods. Early researchers explored whether symbolic reasoning could be represented computationally. They attempted to build systems that manipulated symbols according to formal rules. These systems could solve certain logical problems, play games, and perform tasks in carefully controlled environments. However, many early systems struggled when they encountered uncertainty, incomplete information, or the enormous complexity of the real world.

During the twentieth century, researchers developed programs designed to perform tasks such as theorem proving, game playing, and language processing. Some systems were based on explicit knowledge represented as rules. An expert system, for example, could contain a collection of rules written by human specialists. The system would examine facts and apply rules to reach a conclusion. Expert systems were useful in narrow domains but were difficult to maintain because experts had to manually encode large amounts of knowledge.

The growth of machine learning changed the direction of artificial intelligence. Instead of attempting to write every rule by hand, researchers began developing algorithms that could learn patterns from examples. A machine learning model receives data during training and adjusts internal parameters to improve its predictions. After training, the model can apply the learned patterns to new inputs.

A dataset is a collection of examples used by a machine learning system. The quality of a dataset strongly influences the quality of the resulting model. A dataset may contain text, images, audio, numerical measurements, or other types of information. Data should be sufficiently diverse to represent the situations in which a model will be used. If a model is trained on a narrow or biased dataset, its behavior may not generalize well to new examples.

Machine learning systems commonly divide data into training data and validation or test data. Training data is used to adjust the model's parameters. Validation data is used to evaluate performance during development. Test data is used to measure performance on data that was not used to make development decisions. Keeping evaluation data separate helps researchers determine whether a model has learned general patterns rather than simply memorizing examples.

A machine learning model contains parameters that are adjusted during training. These parameters determine how the model transforms its input into an output. Training begins with parameters that may be randomly initialized or initialized using a previous model. The model produces predictions, and a loss function measures how different those predictions are from the desired targets. An optimization algorithm then modifies the parameters to reduce the loss.

The loss function is an important part of machine learning. For a classification task, a loss function can measure how well the predicted probabilities correspond to the correct class. For language modeling, cross-entropy loss is commonly used to measure how well a model predicts the next token in a sequence. A lower loss generally means that the model assigns higher probability to the correct target tokens.

Gradient descent is one of the fundamental optimization methods used in machine learning. The gradient describes how the loss changes when model parameters change. An optimizer uses gradient information to update the parameters in a direction that is expected to reduce the loss. Modern optimizers such as Adam and AdamW use variations of gradient-based optimization to improve the training process.

A model that performs well on its training data but poorly on unseen data is said to be overfitting. Overfitting occurs when a model learns details that are specific to the training examples rather than general patterns. A model may memorize particular sentences, images, or examples while failing to perform well on new data. Validation loss is useful for detecting this problem.

Underfitting occurs when a model is not powerful enough, has not been trained sufficiently, or has not learned the important patterns in the data. An underfitted model performs poorly on both training data and unseen data. The goal of training is to find a balance in which the model learns useful general patterns without simply memorizing the training examples.

Deep learning is a branch of machine learning based on neural networks with multiple layers. A neural network receives numerical representations of data and applies a series of mathematical transformations. Each layer can learn different types of representations. Early layers may learn simple patterns, while deeper layers can combine those patterns into more complex representations.

A basic neural network contains weights and biases. Weights determine the strength of connections between numerical values, while biases allow the network to shift its computations. During training, the values of the weights and biases are adjusted. The network uses an activation function between layers to introduce nonlinearity. Without nonlinearity, multiple linear transformations could be reduced to a single linear transformation.

The process of calculating how model parameters should change is called backpropagation. During the forward pass, the model processes an input and produces an output. A loss function measures the error. During the backward pass, gradients are calculated by applying the chain rule of calculus through the network. The optimizer then uses those gradients to update the parameters.

The amount of data and computational power available for training has played an important role in the progress of deep learning. Larger datasets can provide more examples of language, images, or other patterns. Larger models can represent more complex relationships. However, simply increasing the size of a model does not guarantee better results. Data quality, training procedures, architecture, evaluation, and the intended task all matter.

Natural language processing, often abbreviated as NLP, is the area of artificial intelligence concerned with human language. NLP systems can perform tasks such as text classification, translation, summarization, information extraction, question answering, speech recognition, and text generation.

Computers do not naturally process words in the same way that humans do. Text must be converted into numerical representations. Tokenization is the process of dividing text into tokens. A token may represent a complete word, part of a word, punctuation, or another unit of text. The tokenizer maps tokens to numerical token IDs that can be processed by a neural network.

A language model learns statistical patterns in sequences of tokens. In next-token prediction, the model receives a sequence of tokens and attempts to predict the token that comes next. For example, given the sequence "Artificial intelligence is", a language model may assign probabilities to possible next tokens such as "a", "important", or "used". During training, the model compares its predictions with the actual next token and updates its parameters.

Large language models are neural networks trained on very large collections of text. Many modern language models use the Transformer architecture. A Transformer processes token representations using attention mechanisms and neural network layers. The model learns relationships between tokens and can use information from different parts of a sequence when making predictions.

Attention allows a model to assign different levels of importance to different tokens. When processing a sequence, a token may need to consider information from earlier words. Attention provides a mechanism for computing relationships between representations. Self-attention allows tokens within the same sequence to interact with one another.

The Transformer architecture typically includes token embeddings, positional information, attention layers, feed-forward neural networks, normalization, and residual connections. Token embeddings convert token IDs into vectors. Positional information gives the model information about the order of tokens. Attention layers process relationships between tokens. Feed-forward layers transform each token representation through additional learned computations.

Generative artificial intelligence refers to systems that can create new content. Generative models can produce text, images, audio, video, computer code, and other forms of content. A language model generates text by repeatedly predicting the next token and adding the selected token to the sequence.

Text generation is probabilistic. At each step, the model produces a probability distribution over possible next tokens. A generation system can choose the most probable token, sample from the distribution, or use strategies that restrict the possible choices. Different generation methods can produce more deterministic or more diverse results.

Temperature is one method used to control the randomness of text generation. A lower temperature makes the probability distribution more concentrated and usually produces more predictable text. A higher temperature makes the distribution more spread out and can produce more diverse text. Very high temperatures may produce incoherent results.

Top-k sampling restricts the next-token choices to the k tokens with the highest probabilities. Top-p sampling instead selects a group of tokens whose cumulative probability reaches a specified threshold. These methods can reduce unlikely and low-quality token choices while retaining some randomness.

Computer vision is the field of artificial intelligence concerned with processing visual information. Computer vision systems can classify images, detect objects, segment regions, recognize faces, estimate depth, and analyze video. Images are represented numerically, often as arrays of pixel values. Neural networks can learn visual features from these numerical representations.

Convolutional neural networks became important in computer vision because convolution operations can detect local patterns in images. Early layers may respond to edges and simple shapes, while deeper layers can recognize more complex structures. Modern vision systems also use Transformer-based architectures and other neural network designs.

Reinforcement learning is a machine learning approach in which an agent interacts with an environment. The agent observes a state, selects an action, and receives a reward or penalty. Over time, the agent attempts to learn a strategy that maximizes the total reward. Reinforcement learning has been used in games, robotics, control systems, and other decision-making problems.

A reinforcement learning system must balance exploration and exploitation. Exploration means trying actions that may provide new information. Exploitation means choosing actions that are already believed to produce good results. A system that only exploits may miss better strategies, while a system that explores too much may fail to use what it has already learned.

Artificial intelligence is used in many industries. Healthcare systems can assist with medical image analysis and research. Financial institutions use machine learning for fraud detection and risk analysis. Manufacturing systems can predict equipment failures. Transportation systems use AI for route planning and perception. Businesses use language models for search, customer support, document analysis, and content generation.

An AI system does not necessarily need to be trained from scratch for every application. A pretrained model can be adapted to a particular task. Fine-tuning continues training a pretrained model on a more specialized dataset. This can help the model learn a particular style, domain, or task.

Retrieval-augmented generation combines a language model with an information retrieval system. Instead of requiring a model to memorize all information in its parameters, a retrieval system searches a collection of documents for relevant information. The retrieved information is then provided to the language model as context. This approach can help a system answer questions using external or private knowledge.

Artificial intelligence systems have important limitations. A model can produce incorrect information with high confidence. A language model generates outputs based on learned statistical patterns and does not automatically guarantee that every statement is true. Systems should therefore be evaluated carefully, especially when used in situations where errors can have serious consequences.

Bias can also appear in artificial intelligence systems. If training data contains biases or does not represent certain groups adequately, a model may reproduce or amplify those patterns. Reducing harmful bias requires careful dataset design, evaluation, monitoring, and responsible deployment.

Privacy is another important concern. Data used to train or operate an AI system may contain sensitive information. Organizations should consider how data is collected, stored, processed, and protected. Systems should avoid exposing private information and should follow applicable laws and policies.

AI safety involves reducing harmful behavior and improving the reliability of artificial intelligence systems. Safety work can include testing, monitoring, access controls, model evaluations, human oversight, and methods for making systems more robust. The appropriate safety measures depend on the capabilities and intended use of the system.

The future development of artificial intelligence will likely involve improvements in models, datasets, hardware, algorithms, and interfaces. Researchers continue to investigate how systems can reason, learn efficiently, use tools, interact with environments, and work with different types of information. Progress in artificial intelligence depends not only on building larger models but also on understanding how to make systems reliable, useful, efficient, and safe.
