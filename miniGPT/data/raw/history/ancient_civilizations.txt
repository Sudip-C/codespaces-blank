Ancient civilizations developed when human communities began creating permanent settlements, organized governments, complex economies, writing systems, monumental architecture, and specialized occupations.

The development of agriculture was one of the most important changes in human history.

When people learned to cultivate crops and domesticate animals, some communities became more settled.

Permanent settlements allowed populations to grow.

Agricultural surpluses supported people who performed occupations other than farming.

Administrators, craftsmen, merchants, soldiers, builders, and religious specialists became increasingly important.

The earliest civilizations developed in several different regions of the world.

Mesopotamia was one of the earliest regions to develop cities and complex states.

The name Mesopotamia means the land between rivers.

The Tigris and Euphrates rivers supported agriculture in the region.

Flooding could provide fertile soil but could also be unpredictable.

Irrigation systems helped farmers control water for agriculture.

Sumer was one of the earliest major civilizations of Mesopotamia.

Sumer was organized into independent city-states.

Important Sumerian cities included Uruk, Ur, and Lagash.

Each city-state had its own government and political institutions.

Temples played important religious and economic roles.

The Sumerians developed cuneiform writing.

Cuneiform began as a system for recording economic transactions.

It later became capable of recording laws, literature, religious texts, and historical information.

The Epic of Gilgamesh is one of the best-known works of ancient Mesopotamian literature.

The Sumerians made contributions to mathematics and astronomy.

They used systems based on sixty.

The division of an hour into sixty minutes and a minute into sixty seconds is connected to ancient mathematical traditions.

The Akkadian Empire was established by Sargon of Akkad.

It united many Mesopotamian city-states under centralized rule.

The empire eventually declined.

The Babylonian Empire later became a major power in Mesopotamia.

Hammurabi was a famous Babylonian ruler.

The Code of Hammurabi contains laws and penalties.

The laws reveal aspects of social hierarchy, property, family relationships, labor, and justice in ancient Babylonian society.

The Assyrian Empire became a powerful military state.

The Assyrians controlled large areas of the ancient Near East.

They developed military technologies and administrative systems.

The Neo-Babylonian Empire later became a major regional power.

Babylon was an important political and cultural center.

Mesopotamian civilizations influenced later societies through writing, law, mathematics, government, and urban organization.

Ancient Egypt developed along the Nile River.

The Nile provided water and fertile agricultural land.

The predictable annual flooding of the Nile supported farming.

Egypt was often divided into Upper Egypt and Lower Egypt.

Political unification created a centralized Egyptian state.

The ruler of Egypt was known as the pharaoh.

Pharaohs were associated with political and religious authority.

Ancient Egyptian society had a complex hierarchy.

Farmers formed a large part of the population.

Craftsmen, scribes, soldiers, priests, administrators, and officials held specialized roles.

Scribes were important because writing was essential to administration.

Egyptian writing included hieroglyphic symbols.

The Egyptians also used other forms of writing for administrative purposes.

The Egyptian writing system recorded religious texts, administrative information, literature, and historical events.

Egyptian religion included many gods and goddesses.

Religious beliefs were closely connected to death and the afterlife.

The Egyptians developed elaborate burial practices.

Mummification was used to preserve bodies in certain contexts.

The pyramids were monumental structures associated with royal burials.

The Great Pyramid of Giza is one of the most famous surviving ancient structures.

The Old Kingdom is often associated with the construction of major pyramids.

The Middle Kingdom was a period of political stability and cultural development.

The New Kingdom was a powerful period in Egyptian history.

Egypt expanded its influence and controlled territories beyond the Nile Valley.

Pharaohs such as Hatshepsut, Akhenaten, Tutankhamun, and Ramesses II are associated with different aspects of Egyptian history.

Hatshepsut was a powerful ruler who supported trade and monumental construction.

Akhenaten promoted a major religious change centered on the worship of Aten.

Tutankhamun became famous because his tomb was discovered with many preserved objects.

Ramesses II ruled for a long period and was associated with major building projects.

Egyptian civilization lasted for thousands of years.

The political power of Egypt changed over time.

Egypt was influenced by neighboring powers including Nubia, Assyria, Persia, Greece, and Rome.

The Nile remained central to Egyptian civilization.

The Indus Valley Civilization developed in parts of South Asia.

It is also called the Harappan Civilization.

Major urban sites included Harappa and Mohenjo-daro.

The civilization developed planned cities.

Some cities had organized streets and sophisticated drainage systems.

Buildings were often constructed using standardized bricks.

The civilization participated in trade with neighboring regions.

Archaeological evidence indicates contact between the Indus Valley and Mesopotamia.

The Indus script has not been conclusively deciphered.

Because the writing system remains undeciphered, many aspects of the civilization remain uncertain.

The political structure of the Indus Valley Civilization is not fully understood.

Archaeological evidence provides information about urban planning, crafts, trade, and daily life.

The civilization declined over time.

Several factors may have contributed to changes in settlement patterns.

Environmental changes, river changes, climate conditions, and economic transformations may have played roles.

The decline was not necessarily a single sudden event.

Ancient China developed along major river systems.

The Yellow River was especially important to early Chinese civilization.

The Xia dynasty is traditionally described as an early Chinese dynasty.

Historical evidence about the earliest dynasties varies.

The Shang dynasty is supported by archaeological and written evidence.

The Shang developed bronze technology and a writing system.

Oracle bones contain inscriptions used for divination and record keeping.

The Zhou dynasty replaced the Shang.

The Zhou developed the idea of the Mandate of Heaven.

According to this political idea, legitimate rulers governed with the approval of Heaven.

A ruler who governed badly could lose this legitimacy.

The Zhou period was long and included significant political changes.

During the later Zhou period, political authority became divided among competing states.

The Warring States period involved intense competition between regional states.

Several philosophical traditions developed during this period.

Confucianism emphasized social relationships, moral behavior, education, and responsible government.

Confucius taught the importance of ethical conduct and proper relationships.

Daoism emphasized harmony with the Dao and natural processes.

Legalism emphasized strict laws and strong centralized authority.

These traditions influenced Chinese culture and politics for centuries.

Qin Shi Huang unified China in 221 BCE.

He became the first emperor of a unified imperial China.

The Qin government used centralized administration.

The Qin dynasty standardized writing, measurements, and currency.

Large construction projects were undertaken during the Qin period.

The Qin dynasty was short-lived.

The Han dynasty followed the Qin.

The Han dynasty became one of the most important periods in Chinese history.

The Han developed a strong centralized state.

The civil service system became increasingly important.

Confucian ideas influenced government administration.

The Han Empire expanded its territory.

Trade routes connected China with Central Asia and other regions.

The Silk Roads developed as networks of trade and cultural exchange.

Silk became an important export from China.

Goods, technologies, religions, and ideas traveled along these networks.

The Persian Empire became one of the largest empires of the ancient world.

The Achaemenid Empire was founded by Cyrus the Great.

Cyrus conquered a large territory while often allowing local customs and traditions to continue.

Darius I expanded and organized the empire.

The Persian Empire developed administrative systems to govern a vast territory.

The empire was divided into provinces called satrapies.

Royal roads improved communication and administration.

The Persian Empire supported trade across its territories.

Persian rulers built monumental structures at places such as Persepolis.

The Persian Empire came into conflict with Greek city-states.

The Greco-Persian Wars included conflicts such as the Battle of Marathon and the Battle of Thermopylae.

The Persian Empire was eventually conquered by Alexander the Great.

Ancient Greece was not a unified nation-state in the modern sense.

It consisted of many independent city-states.

The city-state was called a polis.

Athens and Sparta were two of the most famous Greek city-states.

Athens developed a form of direct democracy.

Only certain citizens could participate in political decision-making.

Women, enslaved people, and foreigners were excluded from Athenian citizenship.

Sparta developed a society focused heavily on military discipline.

Spartan society depended on a large population of enslaved laborers.

Greek mythology included gods such as Zeus, Athena, Apollo, and Aphrodite.

Religious festivals and temples were important parts of Greek society.

The Olympic Games originated in ancient Greece.

Greek philosophers asked questions about nature, knowledge, ethics, and politics.

Socrates used questioning as a method of philosophical inquiry.

Plato founded the Academy and wrote philosophical dialogues.

Aristotle studied philosophy, science, politics, biology, and logic.

Greek mathematicians made important contributions to geometry.

Greek scientists studied astronomy and the natural world.

Greek drama included tragedy and comedy.

Greek architecture influenced later civilizations.

The Parthenon is an important example of classical Greek architecture.

Alexander the Great became king of Macedon.

He conquered the Persian Empire and created a large empire extending into parts of Asia.

Alexander's conquests spread Greek language and culture.

The Hellenistic period followed Alexander's conquests.

Greek culture interacted with Egyptian, Persian, and other traditions.

Alexandria in Egypt became an important center of learning.

The Library of Alexandria became associated with scholarship and the preservation of texts.

Ancient Rome began as a settlement in the Italian Peninsula.

Rome developed into a republic.

The Roman Republic had institutions including elected officials and a senate.

Roman political power was not equally distributed among all people.

Social conflicts occurred between patricians and plebeians.

Roman law developed over time.

The Twelve Tables were an early written collection of Roman laws.

Rome expanded through military conquest.

The Roman army was an important institution.

Roman roads supported military movement, trade, and communication.

Rome eventually controlled much of the Mediterranean region.

Julius Caesar became a powerful Roman military and political leader.

His rise contributed to the end of the Roman Republic.

After a period of civil war, Augustus became the first Roman emperor.

The Roman Empire developed a centralized imperial system.

Roman cities included public buildings, roads, aqueducts, baths, and marketplaces.

Aqueducts transported water over long distances.

Roman engineering produced large bridges, roads, and buildings.

Roman law influenced later legal traditions.

Latin was widely used in the western Roman world.

Greek remained important in the eastern Mediterranean.

Christianity emerged within the Roman Empire.

Early Christians sometimes faced persecution.

The religion gradually spread throughout the empire.

Emperor Constantine supported Christianity.

Christianity later became a major religion of the Roman Empire.

The Roman Empire experienced political, economic, military, and social challenges.

The empire was divided administratively.

The Eastern Roman Empire continued after the western empire declined.

The Byzantine Empire was centered on Constantinople.

The Byzantine Empire preserved Roman political traditions and Greek cultural influences.

The Byzantine emperor Justinian supported a major compilation of Roman law.

The Hagia Sophia was built during the reign of Justinian.

The Byzantine Empire remained an important power for centuries.

The Maya civilization developed in Mesoamerica.

Maya societies built cities, temples, and monuments.

The Maya developed a writing system.

Maya scholars studied astronomy and mathematics.

The Maya used a sophisticated calendar system.

The concept of zero was used in Maya mathematics.

Maya civilization was politically divided among different city-states.

Some city-states competed and fought with one another.

Maya civilization experienced major changes over time.

The decline of some major cities did not mean the disappearance of Maya peoples.

Maya communities continue to exist today.

The Aztec Empire developed in central Mexico.

The Aztec capital Tenochtitlan was built on an island in Lake Texcoco.

Tenochtitlan became a large and powerful urban center.

The Aztec Empire expanded through military conquest and alliances.

The Aztec economy included agriculture, tribute, trade, and markets.

Chinampas were agricultural systems used in the lake environment.

Aztec religion included multiple gods.

The Aztec Empire was conquered by Spanish forces and their Indigenous allies in the sixteenth century.

Disease and political alliances played important roles in the conquest.

The Inca Empire developed in the Andes Mountains.

The Inca created the largest empire in pre-Columbian South America.

Cusco was the political center of the Inca Empire.

The Inca built extensive roads across difficult mountainous terrain.

The road network supported communication, administration, and military movement.

The Inca used administrative systems to govern a large territory.

Terrace agriculture allowed farming in mountainous regions.

The Inca did not use a conventional writing system like those of some other ancient civilizations.

They used quipu systems involving knotted cords for recording information.

The Inca Empire was weakened by internal conflict before the arrival of Spanish forces.

Spanish conquest transformed the political and social structure of the Andes.

Ancient civilizations developed independently in different regions.

They created different languages, religions, political institutions, technologies, and cultural traditions.

At the same time, civilizations interacted through trade, migration, warfare, and cultural exchange.

Ancient history demonstrates that complex societies developed in many parts of the world.

The study of ancient civilizations depends heavily on archaeological evidence.

Written records provide valuable information but are often incomplete.

Archaeological evidence can reveal information about ordinary people who were not mentioned in historical texts.

Artifacts, buildings, tools, bones, pottery, inscriptions, and environmental evidence help reconstruct the past.

Ancient civilizations were diverse and constantly changing.

Their achievements influenced later societies in law, government, architecture, mathematics, writing, science, engineering, and culture.

Understanding ancient civilizations helps explain the development of the modern world.
